Integrative Multi-Omics Prioritization of Therapeutic Targets for Sensorineural Hearing Loss

Omer Ozmen, et al.
Department of Translational Medicine, Uludag University, Türkiye Institute
Abstract

Background: Sensorineural hearing loss affects millions worldwide, yet identifying optimal therapeutic targets remains challenging due to the complexity of inner ear biology. Existing approaches often lack integration across development, aging, and species conservation.

Methods: We developed a Therapeutic Priority Score (TPS) pipeline integrating single-nucleus RNA-seq data from human (GSE213796) and mouse (GSE114157, GSE60019, GSE274279) inner ears. We scored genes based on hair cell specificity, developmental peak expression, aging-related decline, cross-species conservation, and GWAS support. We further integrated noise-injury response data and druggability metrics (pLI, localization) to refine the candidate list.

Results: Our analysis prioritized 72,655 genes, identifying OTOF, SLC26A5, and MYO7A as top therapeutic candidates. We validated the pipeline using known deafness genes, achieving 100% recall for positive controls in the top 100. Cell-type specific analysis revealed distinct target profiles for hair cells, supporting cells, and spiral ganglion neurons. Pathway enrichment analysis for the top 50 candidates did not yield statistically significant terms (FDR < 0.05), suggesting a functional diversity among the prioritized targets.

Conclusion: This study provides a robust, data-driven framework for prioritizing hearing loss targets. The resulting atlas and TPS rankings offer a valuable resource for accelerating the development of gene therapies and small molecule interventions.

Introduction

Sensorineural hearing loss (SNHL) is a prevalent sensory deficit, yet pharmacological treatments remain elusive. The inner ear's complexity, with its diverse cell types and intricate developmental programs, poses a significant barrier to target identification. While individual datasets exist, a unified framework integrating human and mouse data across lifespan is lacking.

In this study, we present a comprehensive multi-omics atlas and a novel Therapeutic Priority Score (TPS) to systematically rank potential targets. By synthesizing data from development, maturation, and aging, we aim to identify genes that are not only essential for hearing but also amenable to therapeutic intervention.

Methods

Data Acquisition and Processing

We obtained public single-cell and bulk RNA-seq datasets from GEO:

  • Human: GSE213796 (Kelley et al.), comprising fetal and adult inner ear samples.
  • Mouse Mature: GSE114157 (Yamashita et al.), sorted hair cells.
  • Mouse Development: GSE60019 (Scheffer et al.), bulk RNA-seq time series (E16-P7).
  • Mouse Aging: GSE274279 (Xia et al., 2025), aging cochlea and utricle.

Raw counts were processed using Scanpy (v1.9). Data were log-normalized (log1p) and scaled. Cross-species integration was performed using ortholog mapping via Ensembl Biomart.

Therapeutic Priority Score (TPS) Calculation

We defined the TPS as a weighted sum of five biological metrics:

  1. HC Specificity: Difference in mean expression between hair cells and non-sensory cells in the human atlas.
  2. Developmental Peak: 99th percentile expression during mouse development, prioritizing genes essential for maturation.
  3. Aging Decline: Fold-change difference between young and aged mouse samples.
  4. Conservation: Correlation of expression profiles between human and mouse orthologs.
  5. GWAS Support: Binary indicator based on curated hearing loss loci.

The score was calculated as a weighted sum. Advanced integration incorporated noise injury response and druggability metrics (pLI scores, subcellular localization).

Results

A Multi-Species Atlas of the Inner Ear

We successfully integrated 4 datasets spanning human and mouse lineages. UMAP visualization revealed distinct clusters corresponding to hair cells (IHC, OHC), supporting cells, and neurons, conserved across species.

Identification of High-Priority Therapeutic Targets

The TPS pipeline ranked 72,655 genes. The top candidates included well-established deafness genes:

  • OTOF (Rank 1): High specificity and conservation, critical for synaptic transmission.
  • SLC26A5 (Prestin, Rank 2): Essential for OHC electromotility.
  • MYO7A (Rank 8): Validated Usher syndrome gene.

Validation against a curated list of positive controls showed 100% recall in the top 100 genes.

Cell-Type Specific Vulnerabilities

Deconvolution of scores by cell type identified distinct target sets:

  • Hair Cells: Enriched for MYO6, PTPRQ, TMC1.
  • Supporting Cells: Enriched for GATA3, FGFR3.
  • SGN: Enriched for NEFL, TUBB3.

Mechanisms of Aging and Injury

Integration of aging data highlighted genes with significant expression decline in aged mice, suggesting potential targets for age-related hearing loss (ARHL). Noise-injury integration prioritized genes like KCNQ4 that are downregulated after trauma, pointing to rescue opportunities.

Biological Modules

We refined the prioritization using specific biological modules:

  • HC Regulatory Module: Correlation with key drivers (Atoh1, Pou4f3, Gfi1).
  • SC Plasticity Module: Correlation with regeneration markers (Sox2, Hes1, Lgr5).
  • SGN Vulnerability Module: Specificity to spiral ganglion neurons and noise sensitivity.
  • Human Transferability: Conservation in human organoid atlas.

Discussion

Our study presents a comprehensive framework for prioritizing hearing loss targets. Unlike previous studies that focus on single datasets, our TPS metric integrates specificity, development, aging, and conservation. The high ranking of OTOF and SLC26A5 validates our approach, while the identification of novel targets warrants further experimental validation.

Limitations include the use of bulk RNA-seq for developmental stages. Future work will incorporate real-time AAV transduction efficiency data to further refine gene therapy suitability.

Conclusion

The TPS framework provides a prioritized roadmap for hearing restoration therapies. By making these results accessible via an interactive web dashboard, we aim to accelerate the translation of genomic discoveries into clinical treatments.
